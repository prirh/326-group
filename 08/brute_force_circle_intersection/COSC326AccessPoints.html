Access Points

RHIANNE PRICE, ERINA JEFFERY, ZAC GARDNER, CALEB MITCHELL

Our approach to solving access points is a brute force search with some shortcuts, combined with a geometric check.

GEOMETRIC CHECK

Given a set of points P and a radius r, let the depth of any point pi in P be equal to the maximum number of circles of radius r centered on each point in P that intersect on the surface of the circle of radius r centered on pi. In a three point P this would look like:

The depth of p1 is 2, p2 has a depth of 1, and p3 has a depth of 2.

The problem in this etude is to find the largest circle such that the deepest point in P has a depth of 11. We have developed a system to find the depth of the deepest point in P given an r:

Draw a circle of radius r centered on each point in P. For each of these circles ci  in C:

  1. Find the depth of the leftmost point on ci: depth of the leftmost point is equal to the number of points in P whose distance to the leftmost point of ci is less than or equal to r.
  2. Find all circles in C that intersect with ci. These will be the circles centred on points from P whose distance to pi is less than or equal to r.
  3. For each of the circles in C that intersect ci, find the points of intersection, making note of whether the intersection is an entry point or exit point to the other circle. Sort all the points in the top half (y > pi.y) in increasing order of x, and Sort all the points in the bottom half(y < pi.y) in decreasing order of x to give all the intersection points around ci in clockwise order from the left most point.
  4. Starting with the depth of the leftmost point from (1) Iterate over the intersections, adding one to the depth if an intersection is an entry point, and decrementing for exits, keeping track of the maximum depth at any intersection on this circle. The maximum is the depth of pi for this r.

For this given r, the deepest point is the one that had the largest depth calculated in this way. The depth of the deepest point in P strictly increases with r.

WHICH R TO CHECK?

Given that we can find the depth of the deepest point in P given an r, we just need to choose a set of R to try. At first we considered taking half the distance from each pair of points in P, but you just have to draw 12 points on the surface of a circle where no two are diametrically opposite to prove this wrong.

We then considered taking the radii of the circles that each set of three points defines (given they aren’t colinear), as it seemed like the maximum circle has to lie on three points in P. This is not strictly true because the circle is defined as the smallest circle that encloses exactly 11 points. Say you have enclosed the tightest set of 10 points with 3 of those 10 on the surface of your circle. Say the 11th member of of the tightest set of 11 lie outside this circle, your new circle won’t necessarily be defined by these three points, as you can make an even smaller circle by letting the first of the three fall on the interior. Since the circle needs to be able to be placed anywhere, it has to be the smallest circle that encloses 11 points.

BRUTE FORCE SEARCH

My first thought was to choose a sensible starting point for r, check the depth of the deepest point by running our depth procedure, and decrement r until the depth of the deepest point was equal to 11,  since the deepest point’s depth decreases with r. Starting high and decrementing guarantees the largest legal circle. This would be hugely inefficient for any reasonable level of accuracy over a large range. Our final solution has this search at it’s core but is slightly better.

The first improvement is to perform a binary search for the radius that sets the maximum depth to 12 rather than 11, and use that radius as the starting point for the search.

The deepest point is alway the deepest point, at any radius. Instead of checking the depth of all points to find the deepest for every r, we can do this once with our starting radius to narrow down P to contain only the deepest points for all subsequent radius checks.

We have tested our strategy on obvious cases such as points around a circle, points on a line, 11 identical points, 10 identical points and 1 other point, and our implementation behaves as expected. We have also run our code on larger sets of randomly dispersed points, but it is hard to determine how the programme should behave in these cases. We have a testing procedure that draws a circle with a radius given by the solution, and places it at 1000 x 1000 different spots on the grid and checks how many points from P are enclosed. This testing strategy is guaranteed to identify incorrect solutions, but cannot identify the correct solutions. A rough way to test this is to increment the radius of the test circle and rerun the test.

ACCURACY

In our search we define R as r’s uniformly distributed between 0 and a maximum radius with a gap of 10e-4. Our maximum radius is more than half of the distance between the two furthest points. This narrows down the answer to 4 decimal places which is accurate enough. We drop this down to 2 dp with 10e-2 if the maximum distance between the two furthest points is larger than 500, just because the search takes too long otherwise.
